### 1 Structured Paper Summary

*   **Introduction:** The paper addresses whether intact, Jupiter-sized planets can survive in close orbits (P ~ days) around white dwarfs (WDs), a phase reached after a star's destructive red giant expansion. Prior evidence existed for debris (tidally disrupted asteroids, gaseous disks) but no intact planets. The discovery of a transiting candidate around WD 1856+534 tests survival and migration mechanisms.
    
*   **Methods:** A multi-tiered approach was used:
    
    1.  **Detection:** TESS 2-minute cadence photometry identified a ~1.4-day, 8-minute transit signal.
        
    2.  **Validation:** High-resolution ground-based photometry (private scopes, GTC) confirmed the transit depth (~56%) and ruled out false positives.
        
    3.  **Characterization:** Multi-wavelength spectroscopy classified the host as a featureless DC white dwarf, preventing radial velocity mass measurement. Spitzer/IRAC 4.5 µm observations were used to constrain the companion's thermal emission.
        
    4.  **Analysis:** Transit parameters were fitted with MCMC. The companion's mass was constrained by comparing the Spitzer flux upper limit to brown dwarf/planet evolutionary models (Sonora, MESA). Formation scenarios (common-envelope vs. dynamical scattering) were evaluated analytically and with N-body simulations (Mercury6).
        
*   **Results:**
    
    1.  Discovery of WD 1856+534 b: a Jupiter-sized object ( $R_p \sim 10.4 R_\oplus$ ) transiting a ~5.85 Gyr-old white dwarf every 1.40794 days.
        
    2.  A 95% confidence mass upper limit of <14  $M_J$  (likely <11.7  $M_J$  given Galactic kinematics), placing it firmly in the planetary regime.
        
    3.  The long period and low mass make common-envelope formation energetically difficult. The favored formation pathway is **dynamical scattering** by unseen perturbers, throwing the planet into a high-eccentricity orbit later circularized by tides.
        
*   **Discussion:** The results demonstrate that giant planets can avoid tidal destruction during stellar evolution and migrate into close orbits. This motivates searches for smaller, Earth-sized planets around white dwarfs. The dynamical scattering mechanism is agnostic to planet size. Limitations include the grazing transit geometry (degenerate parameters) and model-dependent mass limits.
    
*   **Conclusion:** WD 1856+534 b is a strong planetary candidate. Future transmission spectroscopy with JWST or refined thermal flux measurements can confirm its planetary nature and probe its atmosphere. Its existence proves that dynamical processes can deliver planets to close-in orbits around WDs.
    

### 2 Methodological Deep Dive

*   **Simplified Framework:** The core methodology is a **"detection-validation-characterization-formation"** chain. Transit photometry finds the signal; high-resolution imaging/astrometry validates it's on the WD; multi-wavelength flux measurements bypass the lack of spectroscopy to constrain mass; finally, theoretical models test formation hypotheses. The choice of Spitzer to measure (or limit) thermal emission was critical because optical spectra were unusable for Doppler mass measurement.
    
*   **Key Technical Details:**
    
    *   **Data Pipelines:** TESS SPOC pipeline for light curve extraction and transit search; POET pipeline for Spitzer photometry and systematics correction.
        
    *   **Transit & Mass Modeling:** Used MCMC fitting with analytic transit models, priors on stellar density, and limb-darkening coefficients from WD atmosphere models. The mass limit derives from comparing the observed 4.5 µm flux upper limit  $F_{\text{WD1856 b}} < 10.2 \ \mu\text{Jy} $  (95% conf.) to grids of cooling models (e.g., Sonora) for objects aged 5.85-13.8 Gyr.
        
    *   **Formation Analysis:** For common-envelope, they calculated the required energy efficiency parameter  $\alpha_{\text{CE}}$  using standard formalism (e.g., Equation 5:  $\frac{G M_p M_e}{\lambda_{\text{CE}} R_a} = \alpha \left[ \frac{G M_{\text{wd}} M_{\text{com}}}{2 a_f} - \frac{G M_p M_{\text{com}}}{2 a_i} \right]$ ). For dynamical scattering, they used N-body simulations (Mercury6) of multi-planet systems and calculated tidal circularization timescales using equilibrium tide theory (Equation 12:  $t_{\text{circ}} \propto a^6 Q_p / (k_{2p} M_p R_p^5)$ ).
        
    *   **Orbital Analysis:** Applied the LOFTI algorithm to Gaia astrometry to constrain the wide-orbit parameters of the stellar companions (G 229-20 A/B).
        

### 3 Critical Evaluation

*   **Strengths:**
    
    1.  **Comprehensive Follow-up:** The paper leaves few stones unturned, using spectroscopy, multi-site photometry, Spitzer, and archival data to robustly validate the signal and characterize the host star.
        
    2.  **Innovative Mass Constraint:** In the absence of radial velocity data, using the _lack_ of infrared flux to set a stringent mass limit is a clever and effective approach.
        
    3.  **Rigorous Formation Comparison:** The quantitative analysis of the common-envelope energy budget (showing  $\alpha_{\text{CE}}$  requirements >>1) strongly disfavors that scenario, making the dynamical argument more compelling.
        
*   **Limitations:**
    
    1.  **Grazing Transit Geometry:** As noted, the transit is grazing ( $b \sim 0.7$ ), leading to strong degeneracies between  $R_p/R_*$ , inclination, and  $a/R_*$ . The radius (and thus density) of the planet is poorly constrained, especially if eccentricity is allowed.
        
    2.  **Model-Dependent Mass Limit:** The <14  $M_J$  limit relies on evolutionary/atmospheric models (Sonora). While they test different grids (ATMO 2020), effects like clouds or non-solar composition could alter the mass-temperature-luminosity relation.
        
    3.  **Speculative Dynamical History:** The favored scattering scenario is plausible and well-argued but not directly proven. The existence and properties of the hypothesized perturber(s) are unknown.
        
*   **Evidence Robustness:** The core detection and planetary nature are robust. The validation chain rules out most false positives. The mass limit, while model-dependent, is consistent across different model grids and is fundamentally anchored by a clear non-detection in Spitzer data. The rejection of common-envelope evolution is based on standard theory and energetics, making it a strong argument.

### 5 Key Terminology & References

*   **Key Technical Terms:**
    
    *   **Common-Envelope Evolution:** A short-lived phase where a giant star engulfs a companion; friction shrinks the orbit, potentially ejecting the envelope and leaving a close binary.
        
    *   **Dynamical Scattering:** Gravitational interactions between multiple planets that can eject one or scatter another into a highly eccentric orbit.
        
    *   **Transit Time Variation (TTV):** Deviations from a strictly periodic transit ephemeris caused by gravitational perturbations from other bodies.
        
    *   **Grazing Transit:** A transit where the companion does not fully pass across the stellar disk, complicating parameter determination.
        
    *   **DC White Dwarf:** A spectral class for WDs with a featureless continuum due to a helium-dominated atmosphere with no strong lines.
