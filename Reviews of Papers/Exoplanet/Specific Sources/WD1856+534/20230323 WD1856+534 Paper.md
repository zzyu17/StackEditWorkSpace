### 1 Structured Paper Summary

**Introduction:** The paper addresses the mystery of how the Jupiter-sized planet WD 1856 b arrived at its current 1.4-day orbit around a white dwarf. A key proposed migration mechanism is high-eccentricity migration driven by gravitational interactions with unseen, long-period planets. The paper's objective is to search for and constrain such planets by analyzing Transit Timing Variations (TTVs) of WD 1856 b.

**Methods:** The core methodology is a forward-modeling TTV analysis. The authors compiled a dataset of 41 transit times from multiple sources: high-precision measurements from 8-10m class telescopes (GTC, Gemini) and temporal-coverage measurements from smaller telescopes (e.g., HAO). They used the `ttvfast` N-body integrator to simulate TTVs induced by a wide range of hypothetical companion planets, parameterized by mass ( $m_c$ ), orbital period ( $P_c$ ), eccentricity ( $e_c$ ), and inclination ( $i_c$ ). Crucially, they added an analytic Rømer (light travel time) delay correction, which is significant for long-period companions. Model likelihood was assessed via a  $\chi^2$  comparison between observed and simulated TTVs, with parameter space explored via grid searches.

**Results:** The primary finding is **no statistically significant evidence** for TTVs or additional planets. Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) strongly favors a single-planet model over any two-planet model. The analysis places constraints: it can rule out companions more massive than  $\sim2 M_J$  with periods  $P_c \lesssim 500$  days, and companions more massive than  $\sim10 M_J$  with  $P_c \lesssim 1000$  days, assuming coplanar, circular orbits. Constraints weaken significantly for high eccentricity, high mutual inclination, or longer periods ( $>1200$  days). The high-precision transit times also refined WD 1856 b's ephemeris to a period of  $1.407939217 \pm 0.000000016$  days.

**Discussion:** The null result does not rule out planet-planet scattering as a migration pathway, because any surviving perturbing planets would likely have orbital periods >10 years, beyond the sensitivity of the current ~431-day observational baseline. The authors note that constraints are dominated by the sparse, ultra-precise data from large telescopes, with small-telescope data adding only marginal value.

**Conclusion:** Current TTV data show no signs of additional planets but are only sensitive to periods up to ~4 years. Future observations over the next decade are necessary to probe the orbital period range where planetary perturbers that could have driven high-eccentricity migration are expected to reside.

### 2 Methodological Deep Dive

**Simplified Framework:** The method simulates the gravitational "tug" (and associated light travel time delay) a hypothetical outer planet would exert on WD 1856 b, causing its transits to occur early or late. By systematically testing a vast grid of companion properties and comparing the predicted TTV pattern to the highly precise observed transit times, they can identify which companion parameters are incompatible with the data.

**Key Technical Details:**

*   **Data Synthesis:** A critical step was unifying heterogeneous transit times from different sources (GTC, Gemini, Spitzer, HAO, etc.) onto a consistent timescale (BJD\_TDB) and using a common reference transit (GTC on Oct 22, 2019) to define an integer "transit number."
    
*   **`ttvfast` Adaptation:** The authors had to correct for two limitations of `ttvfast`: (1) its massless-planet approximation, worked around by adding WD 1856 b's assumed mass (10  $M_J$ ) to the stellar mass; (2) its omission of the Rømer delay, for which they implemented an external analytic calculation (Rappaport et al. 2013, Eqns 6 & 7).
    
*   **Likelihood & Parameter Marginalization:** To avoid explicitly fitting the inner planet's period ( $P_b$ ) and reference time ( $t_0$ ) for every trial, they subtracted a best-fit linear trend (in transit number vs. time) from both the model and data before calculating the  $\chi^2$  likelihood ( $\mathcal{L} = e^{-\chi^2/2}$ ). Nuisance parameters (e.g., outer planet's mean anomaly, argument of periastron) were "collapsed" by selecting the value that maximized  $\mathcal{L}$  at each grid point.
    
*   **Grid Search:** Due to computational expense, they used a structured grid search (e.g., 300x100x100 points in mass/period/mean anomaly) rather than Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC), slicing through the 6D parameter space.
    

### 3 Critical Evaluation

**Strengths:**

1.  **Foundational & Direct:** This is the first dedicated TTV study of the benchmark WD 1856 system, providing essential baseline constraints.
    
2.  **Methodological Rigor:** The careful correction of `ttvfast`'s shortcomings (Rømer delay, mass approximation) and the handling of heterogeneous data demonstrate a robust approach.
    
3.  **Transparent Parameter Exploration:** The grid-based approach, while computationally intensive, yields clear, visual "exclusion maps" (e.g., Figures 4, 5, 6) that are more intuitively interpretable than complex posterior distributions from an MCMC for a null result.
    

**Limitations:**

1.  **Inherent Model Assumptions:** Key assumptions limit the generality of the constraints: WD 1856 b's orbit was fixed as circular ( $e_b=0$ ) and its mass was fixed at 10  $M_J$ . Its true, unknown eccentricity (even if small) and mass could affect the dynamics with an outer perturber.
    
2.  **Simplified Dynamics:** The analysis primarily tests for a _single_ additional planet. A more complex, yet plausible, migration history could involve multiple perturbers or a prior scattering event that ejected the perturber, leaving a dynamically "hot" inner planet—a scenario this method cannot detect.
    
3.  **Baseline Limitation (Acknowledged):** The core limitation is the short observational baseline (~1.2 years) relative to the periods of interest (>10 years), which is explicitly stated but fundamentally constrains the study's reach.
    

**Evidence Robustness:** The evidence for the null result is robust within the defined framework. The use of BIC correctly penalizes model complexity, strongly favoring the simpler model. The "allowed" regions in parameter space (green/yellow in figures) are not evidence _for_ planets, but simply indicate a lack of sensitivity. The "spikes" of allowed parameters at specific periods (e.g., ~1 year) are correctly identified as aliasing artifacts due to observational gaps.

### 5 Key Terminology & References

**Key Technical Terms:**

*   **Rømer Delay/Light Travel Time Effect:** The change in observed transit time because the host star itself is moving around the barycenter of the star-planet(s) system. For long-period outer planets, this geometric effect often dominates over dynamical TTVs.
    
*   **Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC):** A criterion for model selection that balances goodness-of-fit ( $\chi^2$ ) with a penalty for the number of parameters.  $\Delta$ BIC > 10 indicates very strong support for the model with the lower BIC.
    
*   **Nuisance Parameter:** A parameter that must be included in the model for completeness but is not of primary scientific interest (e.g., the outer planet's mean anomaly at epoch).
    

**High-Impact References:**

1.  **Vanderburg et al. (2020), _Nature_, 585, 363:** **The discovery paper of WD 1856 b.** Essential for system parameters, initial observations, and discussion of migration hypotheses.
